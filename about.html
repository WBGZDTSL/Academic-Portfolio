Research Background

Who I Am

I am a postdoctoral researcher at the Institute for Computational Linguistics, where I work on the intersection of natural language understanding, structured knowledge representation, and human–AI collaboration. My Ph.D. (Computer Science, 2022) examined how large language models encode and retrieve factual knowledge, with a focus on scientific domains.

My current research investigates efficient adaptation of foundation models to specialized fields — from biomedical literature to legal documents — without sacrificing interpretability or safety. I collaborate with colleagues in cognitive science, linguistics, and information science, believing that robust AI research demands rigorous interdisciplinary engagement.

I have published in venues including ACL, EMNLP, NeurIPS, and ICLR, and serve as a reviewer for TACL, JAIR, and multiple workshops. I also supervise graduate students, teach an advanced seminar on language model reasoning, and contribute to departmental mentorship programs.

Research Interests

Language model reasoning · Scientific knowledge graphs · Parameter-efficient learning · Multimodal document understanding · Evaluation methodology · AI safety and alignment

The Signal & The Noise

Why I Started the Podcast

Academic publishing is rigorous — but it is not always accessible. Peer review, page limits, and disciplinary jargon create barriers that prevent important findings from reaching the people who need them most: policy-makers, practitioners, educators, and fellow researchers working across adjacent fields.

In 2021, I began hosting The Signal & The Noise as an experiment in a different mode of scientific communication. Each episode is a structured conversation — typically 40–50 minutes — with a researcher, practitioner, or thinker whose work I believe deserves wider attention. The format is deliberately unhurried: we discuss methods, motivations, open questions, and the honest ambiguities that papers rarely capture.

The title reflects a conviction I hold about research practice: distinguishing signal (reproducible, generalizable findings) from noise (hype, overfitting, publication bias) is not merely a statistical problem. It is an epistemic and cultural one. The podcast is my contribution to that conversation.

After 40 episodes and conversations with researchers from 14 countries, the project has grown beyond what I expected. I am grateful to everyone who has listened, written in, and shared their own work as a result.
